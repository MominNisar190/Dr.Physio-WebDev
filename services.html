Evidence-based physiotherapy — what the science actually says, and how we apply it.

🖐 Manual TherapyNeurophysiologicalCatalyst, not cure

🖐 Manual Therapy

The Conventional View

Manual therapy — comprising joint mobilizations, spinal manipulations, and deep tissue massage — is traditionally sold as a way to "realign" joints, "break up" scar tissue, or physically release tight fascia to reduce pain and improve mobility.

The Evidence-Based Reality

The mechanical narratives we tell patients about manual therapy are largely false. You cannot physically "break up" scar tissue with your hands; human fascia requires immense force (thousands of pounds) to deform even slightly. Furthermore, joints do not easily go "out of place," so we are not putting them back in.

Instead, manual therapy works via neurophysiological mechanisms. Touch and targeted pressure stimulate mechanoreceptors in the skin and joints. This sends a novel input to the central nervous system, which temporarily down-regulates the perception of threat (pain) and decreases localized muscle guarding. It creates a temporary window of reduced pain and increased mobility.

🩺 Clinical Application: Manual therapy is not a cure — it is a catalyst. It should never be used as a standalone treatment because passive therapies foster patient dependency. The temporary relief gained must immediately be exploited by performing active movement and strength training in that newly acquired, pain-free range of motion.

📚 Source: Mechanisms of Manual Therapy – Pain Medicine

🏃 RehabilitationProgressive OverloadGold Standard

🏃 Rehabilitation Exercises

The Conventional View

Rehabilitation exercises involve personalized plans utilizing resistance bands, bodyweight movements, and stretches to regain strength and function after an injury.

The Evidence-Based Reality

While exercise is the absolute gold standard in physical therapy, the industry suffers from a massive problem: chronic under-dosing. Physiotherapists frequently prescribe exercises that are too light or lack progressive overload out of a misplaced fear of exacerbating symptoms.

To create actual tissue adaptation — whether strengthening a tendon, building muscle, or increasing bone density — the load must exceed the tissue's current capacity. True rehabilitation demands the principles of strength and conditioning: mechanical tension, metabolic stress, and progressive overload.

🩺 Clinical Application: Rehab plans must transition from low-threshold motor control exercises (early stage) to heavy, functional loading (late stage). Pain during rehab exercises is not inherently bad — an acceptable level (3–4/10) during exercise is often necessary and safe, particularly in tendinopathy management.

📚 Source: Progression in Rehabilitation – BJSM

💊 Pain ManagementBiopsychosocialBrain Output

💊 Pain Management

The Conventional View

Pain management revolves around identifying the damaged tissue (the "pain generator") and fixing it through rest, medication, or physical interventions until the pain goes away.

The Evidence-Based Reality

The structural-biomedical model of pain is scientifically obsolete. Pain does not strictly equal tissue damage. Many people have herniated discs or torn rotator cuffs with zero pain, while others have debilitating pain with completely normal MRI scans.

Pain is a complex output generated by the brain, influenced by the Biopsychosocial Model — affected by biology (tissue state), psychology (fear-avoidance, anxiety, beliefs about pain), and sociology (work stress, home environment). Chronic pain represents a sensitized nervous system misinterpreting safe signals as dangerous ones.

🩺 Clinical Application: Effective strategies require cognitive restructuring. Patients must be taught that "hurt does not equal harm." Treatment involves graded exposure — slowly reintroducing feared movements — alongside sleep optimization, stress management, and active movement, rather than relying on NSAIDs or passive rest.

📚 Source: The Lancet Series on Low Back Pain

🏥 Post-SurgicalCriterion-BasedNot Time-Based

🏥 Post-Surgical Rehabilitation

The Conventional View

Following surgery, patients follow a strict time-based protocol (e.g., "At 4 weeks, you can jog; at 6 months, you can play sports").

The Evidence-Based Reality

Time-based healing is an average, not an absolute. Returning a patient to activity simply because "six months have passed" is a primary reason re-injury rates are high (e.g., secondary ACL tears). Rehabilitation must be criterion-based — a patient earns the right to progress only when they pass specific objective tests (limb symmetry index, peak torque, psychological readiness).

🩺 Clinical Application: Safe recovery requires pushing the envelope just enough to stimulate tissue remodeling without disrupting surgical fixation. It includes early mobilization to prevent arthrofibrosis and neuromuscular electrical stimulation (NMES) to prevent severe muscle atrophy in the days immediately following surgery.

📚 Source: Criterion-Based Rehabilitation – JOSPT

⚽ Sports InjuryPEACE & LOVERICE is Dead

⚽ Sports Injury Rehabilitation

The Conventional View

When an athlete is injured, they should immediately apply the R.I.C.E. protocol (Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation) to stop inflammation and heal.

The Evidence-Based Reality

The R.I.C.E. protocol is dead — even its creator (Dr. Gabe Mirkin) has publicly retracted it. Inflammation is the first essential stage of the healing cascade. Ice suppresses the release of macrophages, thereby delaying the removal of cellular debris and actually slowing tissue repair. Absolute "Rest" leads to rapid tissue deconditioning.

The modern standard is P.E.A.C.E. & L.O.V.E. (Protect, Elevate, Avoid anti-inflammatories, Compress, Educate & Load, Optimism, Vascularization, Exercise).

🩺 Clinical Application: Sports rehab requires optimal loading — finding the precise amount of mechanical stress the tissue can handle. It also requires bridging the gap between clinical strength and the chaotic, high-velocity demands of the actual sport through plyometrics, change-of-direction drills, and reactive agility training.

📚 Source: Soft-tissue injuries simply need PEACE and LOVE – BJSM

🧘 PostureMovement VariabilityMyth Busted

🧘 Posture Correction

The Conventional View

"Poor" posture (slouching, text neck, anterior pelvic tilt) causes mechanical strain on the body, leading to long-term musculoskeletal issues and chronic pain.

The Evidence-Based Reality

This is one of the most pervasive myths in healthcare. Decades of high-quality research show almost zero correlation between spinal alignment or "posture" and the presence of pain. The problem is not the shape of the posture, but the duration. Holding any posture — even a perfectly upright one — for 8 hours will cause ischemia and discomfort.

🩺 Clinical Application: Instead of rigidly "correcting" posture, we promote movement variability. The best posture is your next posture. Treatment focuses on building strength and endurance to tolerate different positions, rather than obsessing over perfect alignment.

📚 Source: Fall of the Postural-Structural-Biomechanical Model – JBMT

⚡ ElectrotherapyTENS / UltrasoundUse Sparingly

⚡ Electrotherapy

The Conventional View

Modalities like Ultrasound, TENS, and Interferential Current penetrate deep into tissues to accelerate cellular healing, reduce inflammation, and permanently eliminate pain.

The Evidence-Based Reality

A large portion of electrotherapy is high-tech placebo. Extensive systematic reviews have found that therapeutic ultrasound is largely ineffective for almost all musculoskeletal conditions. TENS can provide acute pain relief via the Gate Control Theory of Pain, but it does not change the biological state of the tissue.

🩺 Clinical Application: Electrotherapy should be relegated to the bottom of the clinical toolbox — useful only as a temporary pain modulator to help a highly irritable patient participate in their active exercise program. Any clinic relying heavily on passive machines is prioritizing low-effort revenue over evidence-based, active recovery.

📚 Source: Effectiveness of TENS and Ultrasound – Cochrane Library

🧍 ErgonomicsWorkplace HealthMove More

🧍 Ergonomic Assessment

The Conventional View

Optimizing the precise angles of a desk, chair, and computer monitor, and teaching workers to lift "with their knees, not their back," will prevent workplace strains and injuries.

The Evidence-Based Reality

Strict ergonomic interventions have largely failed to reduce injury rates in industrial and corporate settings. Disc herniations are more highly correlated with genetics, poor sleep, and abrupt spikes in physical load than with the specific mechanics of a lift.

🩺 Clinical Application: Ergonomic assessments should focus on reducing sustained, unbroken periods of work. Introducing micro-breaks, varying daily tasks, improving cardiovascular fitness, and managing workplace psychosocial stress are vastly more effective than buying a $1,000 ergonomic chair.

📚 Source: Workplace Ergonomic Interventions – Cochrane Review

👴 Geriatric CareHeavy LiftingNo Ageism

👴 Geriatric Physiotherapy

The Conventional View

Seniors are fragile. Treatment should focus on gentle stretching, low-impact seated exercises, and light balance drills to slowly improve mobility and prevent falls.

The Evidence-Based Reality

The standard approach to geriatric physiotherapy is steeped in accidental ageism. Older adults do not lose their ability to adapt to physical stress — they simply adapt slower. Sarcopenia and osteopenia are the primary drivers of falls and loss of independence. You cannot reverse these conditions with 2-pound pink dumbbells.

Older adults safely can, and absolutely must, lift heavy weights. Heavy resistance training is the only proven way to increase bone mineral density and stimulate fast-twitch muscle fibers — the exact fibers required to catch yourself when you trip.

🩺 Clinical Application: Geriatric care should look surprisingly like athletic performance training — heavy compound movements (sit-to-stands holding weights, deadlifts), reactive balance training, and cardiovascular conditioning to combat systemic physical decline.

📚 Source: Resistance Training for Older Adults – NSCA Position Stand
